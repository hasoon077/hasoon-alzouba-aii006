WHAT IS DRIFTING?

NEU DRIFT CLUB

Drifting is a high-skill driving technique where the driver intentionally oversteers, causing the rear wheels to lose traction and the car to slide sideways through corners — all while maintaining full control.

Unlike traditional motorsport where grip is everything, drifting is judged on angle, speed, line, and style. The car is meant to be dancing on the edge of control — that fine line between genius and disaster. Judges and fans reward drivers who push closest to that limit.

HISTORY OF DRIFT 02 / 06
02

BORN IN JAPAN

Drifting was born on the mountain passes of Japan in the 1970s — the touge roads where street racers found that sliding was sometimes faster, and always more spectacular.

1970s
THE TOUGE ORIGINS
Japanese mountain racers discover that controlled oversteer on narrow touge passes gives them a competitive edge. Underground racing culture begins to form around the style.
1988
KEIICHI TSUCHIYA
The legendary "Drift King" releases a video showcasing his drifting techniques on public roads. The tape spreads like wildfire through Japan's car community and officially births drifting as a discipline.
1996
D1 GRAND PRIX FOUNDED
Japan establishes the world's first official drifting competition series. Drifting goes from underground phenomenon to legitimate motorsport with real rules and real champions.
2004
FORMULA DRIFT — USA
North America launches its own championship series. Drifting explodes globally. The Fast & Furious franchise brings drift culture to mainstream audiences worldwide.
TODAY
GLOBAL PHENOMENON
Drift competitions run on every continent. The sport has inspired a generation of drivers including fans right here at NEU — including me.
TECHNIQUES 03 / 06
03

MASTER THE SLIDE

Drifting looks chaotic from the outside. On the inside, it's a precise symphony of throttle, steering, clutch, and weight transfer — all happening in fractions of a second.

01
CLUTCH KICK
The driver rapidly dips and releases the clutch at high RPM to send a sudden shock through the drivetrain, breaking rear traction. It's the most common initiation technique — snappy, loud, and very satisfying.
02
HANDBRAKE ENTRY
Yanking the handbrake locks the rear wheels mid-corner, forcing a rotation. Popular in tight hairpins. Requires perfect timing — too early and you spin, too late and nothing happens.
03
FEINT / SCANDINAVIAN FLICK
The driver sways the car away from the corner, then sharply turns in. The pendulum effect transfers weight and initiates the slide. Elegant, smooth, and extremely fast.
04
POWER OVERSTEER
Pure horsepower overwhelming rear grip. The driver floors the throttle mid-corner and controls the slide purely with steering angle and throttle modulation. Requires serious power and serious skill.
05
TANDEM DRIFT
Two cars sliding together in formation — the follow car must mirror every movement of the lead car at just inches of separation. The most spectacular and dangerous discipline in drifting.

WORLD EVENTS

From the streets of Japan to massive stadium events worldwide, drifting has built a global competition circuit that draws millions of fans every year.

🏁
FORMULA DRIFT USA
The world's premier professional drifting series. Runs across America with stops in Long Beach, Orlando, and Irwindale. 1000hp+ monsters battle for points all season.
🇯🇵
D1 GRAND PRIX — JAPAN
Where it all started. The original drift championship still running in Japan. Judges use a strict scoring system rating line, angle, speed, and showmanship simultaneously.
🇬🇧
BRITISH DRIFT CHAMPIONSHIP
Europe's top drift series. Known for incredible venue variety — from airfields to purpose-built tracks. Produces some of the most technically skilled drivers on the planet.

MY STORY

NEU — North Cyprus

At Near East University, I found my passion. The campus, the cars, the community — everything clicked the moment I watched my first live drift event.

My journey started with watching YouTube compilations late at night — Ken Block's Gymkhana series, Formula Drift highlights, Japanese touge footage. Then I started noticing that some students at NEU were actually drifting in empty lots near campus. The sound of a rear-wheel-drive car being pushed to the limit is something you never forget.

I've learned that drifting teaches you so much more than car control. It teaches you about risk management, physical precision, and reading a surface. Every patch of asphalt is different. Every corner has a character. You learn to feel the car like an extension of your own body.
